Subtheme: Exploring Speculation & Housing for the Rich

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What proportion of high-end Boston real estate is owned by absentee investors and how has this amount changed over time?
    1. Motivation: The Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth article explains that Boston’s high end real estate market has been increasingly dominated by wealthy absentee owners, which include corporate entities and international investors. I think understanding this scale and growth could allow us to fully grasp its impact on the housing supply and affordability.
  2. What is the relationship between income levels and the affordability of Boston’s new housing developments?
    1. The Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth article states that Boston is facing a housing crisis as luxury high-rises continue to be built at a disproportionate rate compared to affordable housing. This question would provide information as to who is benefiting from Boston’s current housing policies
  3. How has private equity and institutional investment in housing affected eviction rates and tenant stability in Boston?
    1. When Private Equity Becomes Your Landlord explains how private equity firms will increase rent while reducing maintenance to push out lower-income tenants all in the name of profit. This question’s goal is to understand how this may be impacting tenant stability, specifically in Boston’s rental market.
  4. How has the concentration of absentee-owned properties affected rent prices in Boston’s neighborhoods?
    1. In the article When Private Equity Becomes Your Landlord, the authors describe how institutional investors prioritize profit over tenant well-being which leads to rising rents. Ultimately this question will investigate this trend, helping us to understand how speculations affect affordability.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Overall this report has highlighted how corporate ownership, speculative real estate investment, and absentee landlords have been altering Boston’s housing market over the last 20 years. The reshaping of this market has also had significant implications on affordability, homeownership and racial equality. From 2004, corporate acquisitions have drastically increased, particularly after 2016 which has the highest concentration in Downtown and Dorchester. Despite this increase, vacancy rates have remained stable which suggest that corporate landlords may be holding properties for speculation rather than making available for residents. These trends have raised concerns about the inflation of housing costs, reduced homeownership opportunities, and potential displacement of long-term residents.

Looking at the racial composition analysis has revealed that Black, Hispanic, and AAPI communities are disproportionately affected by these housing trends. The neighborhoods with higher corporate ownership rates tend to also have lower homeownership rates which reinforce the historical barriers to property ownership for communities of color. Boston’s total housing stock and population have also remained stagnant, which points to corporate investment not increasing housing availability, rather shifting control of existing units. Ultimately the findings suggest that corporate speculation may be driving rent increases, limiting homeownership for marginalized groups, and contributing to an affordability crisis. Further research should be conducted to explore the relationship between rent, income labels, corporate ownership and eviction patterns to highlight the impact on Boston’s housing stability.
